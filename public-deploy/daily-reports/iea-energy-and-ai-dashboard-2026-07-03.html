Key Questions on Energy & AI

AI is the fastest-moving variable in today's energy system. The IEA's 2026 update sees global data-centre electricity consumption nearly doubling — from 485 TWh in 2025 to 945 TWh in 2030 — equivalent to about 3% of worldwide electricity demand. AI-focused data centres triple in the same window. Behind the headlines, the report exposes a stretched supply chain, a shifting power mix (more gas and renewables, a doubling of nuclear), the rise of small modular reactors, and mounting pressure on electricity prices — making this report an essential read for anyone tracking AI's energy footprint.

Q1 Has the IEA adjusted its data-centre demand outlook?

Sector electricity consumption grew by ~15% between 2024 and 2025, adding about 70 TWh to reach almost 500 TWh. Under the updated Base Case, demand roughly doubles from 415 TWh in 2024 to 945 TWh in 2030. The Base Case remains the central scenario; Lift-Off, Headwinds and High-Efficiency cases bracket the range.

Q2 What share of global electricity do data centres consume?

In 2025 data centres accounted for ~1.5% of global electricity; that share rises to ~3% by 2030. They also account for nearly 10% of all electricity-demand growth to 2030 — disproportionate to their current footprint. Within that, AI-focused data centres are responsible for almost all of the additional growth.

Q3 Where are data centres clustered?

Concentration persists and intensifies. The United States hosts roughly half of global installed data-centre capacity. Europe shows the greatest geographic diversification (FLAP-D hubs: Frankfurt, London, Amsterdam, Paris, Dublin), and ~19 of 21 US hyperscale AI sites sit within 65 km of a city of >100 000 people.

Q4 What is the energy-technology mix powering data centres?

Electricity generation for data centres is set to double to >1 000 TWh by 2030. Renewables are the single biggest source of growth (adding >200 TWh). Gas-fired generation also doubles — to around 340 TWh (~30% share) — concentrated in the US. Coal still supplies about 20% globally, mostly in China. Nuclear rises from ~75 TWh to ~120 TWh, helped by SMR offtake deals and tech-company PPAs.

Q5 What is the impact on CO₂ emissions?

Despite rapid growth in renewable PPAs (the tech sector accounts for ~40% of all corporate renewables PPAs signed globally in 2025), the IEA projects data-centre CO₂ emissions to roughly double — reaching ~350 million tonnes by 2035, equal to about 2% of global electricity-sector emissions. The outlook depends heavily on how quickly gas, nuclear and storage scale alongside renewables.

Q6 What cooling technologies are data centres adopting?

AI racks increasingly require liquid cooling as power densities climb and chip power envelopes push past 1 kW per chip (today) toward 5 kW for next-generation Rubin Ultra platforms. Liquids carry heat far more efficiently than air, simplify waste-heat recovery, and unlock higher-density rack designs. Air cooling still dominates legacy installations.

Q7 How is energy split between training and inference?

The centre of gravity of AI energy use has already shifted decisively from training to inference. Agentic AI, video generation and reasoning models consume orders of magnitude more energy per task than a simple text query. The IEA estimates a typical text-only AI query consumes ~0.3 Wh today, while a video-generation request can use hundreds of Wh. Per-task figures continue to fall rapidly, but aggregate demand keeps rising.

Q8 How fast is GPU performance-per-watt improving?

Over the past decade, leading AI processors and accelerators (Nvidia data-centre GPUs P100 → B300 and Google TPUs v2 → v7) have improved performance-per-watt by roughly 30–40% per year on average. Gains are uneven and gradually slowing as transistor scaling matures. Per-task compute needed for a given level of language-model performance has roughly halved every eight months.

Q9 What role will Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) play?

The pipeline of data-centre SMR offtake agreements has nearly doubled, from ~25 GW at end-2024 to 45 GW by end-2025. Operators include Amazon, Google, Microsoft, Equinix, EDF/Holtec/Tritax, Oklo, and many others. Yet the first commercial SMRs are not expected to come online until around 2030 — making near-term impact on the data-centre mix modest, with nuclear generation rising from ~75 TWh (2025) to ~120 TWh (2030).

Q10 What are the policy implications?

Six themes run through the IEA's recommendations — clear data, efficiency-first design, diversified clean power, storage and grid investment, transparent permitting, and international cooperation on supply chains.

Recommendation 01

Increase transparency on data-centre energy use

Mandate systematic disclosures of power consumption, PUE and emissions so planners can see real load growth instead of guessing through connection queues.

Recommendation 02

Maximise efficiency before adding capacity

Pursue software efficiency, hardware efficiency (rightsizing models, newer accelerators) and infrastructure efficiency (better cooling, waste-heat reuse) before commissioning new build-out.

Recommendation 03

Match data-centre growth with clean power PPAs

Push for hourly-matched renewables, expand nuclear PPAs (including SMRs) and reward operators that contract new clean capacity rather than soaking existing zero-carbon generation.

Recommendation 04

Invest in storage, grid and demand-side flexibility

AI loads swing on sub-second timescales. Pair battery storage (20–25 GW expected by 2030 globally), grid-strengthening investments and demand-response to keep reliability at 99.999%.

Recommendation 05

Streamline permitting while engaging communities

Accelerate permitting for strategic infrastructure, but make sure siting decisions address local affordability, environmental and water-use concerns. About 20 projects (USD 98 bn) were blocked or delayed in Q2 2025 alone.

Recommendation 06

Diversify and secure critical supply chains

Turbines, transformers, GPUs and high-bandwidth memory are concentrated bottlenecks. Trade frictions and the 2026 Middle East helium shock show the cost of single-source dependencies.

Q11 What does AI-driven growth mean for energy demand to 2035?

Modelling with the OECD suggests an AI-driven productivity boost could raise global energy demand by 1–4% in 2035 versus a no-AI baseline. Effects are concentrated in advanced economies (especially the United States) because knowledge-intensive services have low energy elasticity. Energy policies and technology choices still matter more than AI itself for the long-run demand trajectory.
