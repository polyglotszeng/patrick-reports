02 · The Great Divergence Framing

Pomeranz's "Great Divergence" thesis (Industrial Revolution caused western economies to outpace the rest) is the report's organizing metaphor. CEA asks: is AI the second Great Divergence?

If the AI revolution is as transformative as the Industrial Revolution, should we expect this to lead to a second Great Divergence? Of course, the future impact of AI is uncertain, so in this paper we focus on the empirical data that can be seen and measured today. — Introduction, page 1

📐 Industrial Revolution analogy

1st Divergence (1750-1900)

Industrializing nations' per-capita GDP pulled away from the rest. Standards of living in the West doubled while China's GDP share collapsed.

Convergence (1900-2025)

Last 25 years saw a partial reversal: developing nations grew faster than rich ones, closing some of the gap.

2nd Divergence hypothesis

If AI diffusion follows investment concentration (U.S. #1, China #2, EU #3), then AI adoption will drive another productivity divergence.

🚨 Pace-of-change caveat

4-5× per year compute scaling

Since 2010 training compute has grown 4-5× per year on average (Epoch AI). Many metrics "double every few months".

What this means

The AI of 2026 will look nothing like the AI of 2024. Forecasting 10-yr GDP impacts from current data is like forecasting 1925 GDP from 1900 railroad data.

Top AI firms

OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI, Meta, Google all publicly aim for AGI or superintelligence. If achieved, the divergence thesis becomes a near-certainty.

📚

Key terms the report defines upfront

Generative AI — produces text/images/video (LLMs are the dominant type). Agentic AI — generative AI that executes actions to accomplish goals. AGI — hypothetical AI matching all human intellectual tasks. ASI — AI that surpasses humans. Specialized intelligence — current state: superhuman at narrow tasks but not generalizable.

🔬 Why the range is so wide

Studies differ on 3 things:

  • Whether AI is mostly a productivity booster vs. labor substitute
  • Whether the gains are concentrated in tech or diffuse across sectors
  • How fast diffusion occurs (corporate adoption vs. individual use)

The 2025 H1 data rules out the very bottom of the range: AI investment alone added +1.3% annualized to GDP, comparable to railroad investment during the Industrial Revolution.

💼 Labor share will fall as AI substitutes

If "AI does everything humans can" (AGI), then capital becomes a substitute for labor → wage growth slows relative to capital returns.

This is the divergence mechanism: AI-leading nations see GDP rise and wages stagnate; AI-laggards see both stagnate.

For the U.S., Acemoglu's +1.5% is plausible if AI augments workers (not substitutes); Aldasoro's +20-45% requires capital-substitution + AGI-like trajectories.

04 · Labor Impact · Jevons' Paradox

Will AI kill jobs or create them? CEA invokes the 1865 Jevons' Paradox — coal-efficient engines increased total coal demand — and argues AI may similarly increase the demand for human labor despite higher productivity.

Jevons' Paradox occurs if a technological advance reduces the amount of a resource (like labor) needed for a specific application. This actually causes overall usage of that resource to increase as usage expands to new applications. — Section 2.4, page 7

📉 Three conditions for Paradox to occur

  1. AI must meaningfully boost worker productivity — evidence supports this (SWE-bench 4%→72% in one year).
  2. Cost savings must translate to lower prices — (still being observed in inference cost: 9×/year cheaper).
  3. Lower prices must increase demand faster than efficiency gains reduce per-unit labor — historical evidence supports this in coal, water, lighting, even road capacity (induced demand).

🏥 Empirical counter-example: radiologists

AI predicted to "replace radiologists" since 2016. Instead, radiologist employment is now at historical highs. Lower cost per diagnosis → more diagnoses demanded → more radiologist hours (not fewer).

🏛️ Historical precedent

1860: 43% of U.S. workforce in agriculture.
2015: 1.2% in agriculture.
Today: majority of workers in jobs created after 1940 (wind turbine tech, software dev, textile chemist, mental-health counselor).

⚠️

The exception risk

Past technologies all created new labor categories. AI could be the exception only if it either (1) develops agency to operate fully independently, OR (2) drives productivity without generating new labor demand (Ayres 1990, Donaldson 2018, Feigenbaum & Gross 2024). The report flags this as the most uncertain lever — most historical analogies say jobs grow, but a few academic models say they don't.

📌 Middle-income AI usage paradox

Middle-income economies account for 50% of global AI usage in 2024 — disproportionately high relative to their share of GDP. Low-income economies: <1% (lack of electricity + data center access). Adoption highly correlated with GDP per capita.

📌 Leapfrogging potential in developing nations

Just as developing countries skipped landlines and went straight to mobile, they may skip centralized data centers and use smartphones as primary AI interaction. Electricity reliability is the main constraint.

12 · Caveats & Blind Spots

Even with the data-heavy framing, the report has limitations worth flagging.

🚨 Stance-driven framing

This is a Trump administration CEA report. The "America-first" lens is everywhere — capital imports, export controls, energy dominance framed as competitive advantage. Not neutral prose.

What's missing

  • No detailed tax incidence analysis (who pays for OBBB)
  • No discussion of wealth concentration from AI
  • Limited treatment of AI safety / alignment risk
  • Treatment of export controls is mostly positive — no mention of long-term blowback

🤷 Forecasting caveat (self-flagged)

The report itself acknowledges:

"Not every AI-related investment will be profitable, and the short-run always contains the potential for substantial volatility."

Why numbers are uncertain

  • Scaling laws could end
  • AI could develop agency and bypass labor demand
  • Adoption curve could plateau at ~10% of production firms (not 78% of org-wide use)
  • Regulatory shocks (tariffs, antitrust) could reroute flows

📚 Citation density

Report has 150 footnotes across 27 pages, dominated by:

  • Stanford HAI AI Index 2025 — ~20 citations (most data)
  • Epoch AI — ~25 citations (compute, performance)
  • CEA staff analysis — policy impact claims
  • Federal Reserve + BLS + Census + IMF — base statistics

Useful as a snapshot of mid-2025 thinking, but derivative of HAI + Epoch.

🔍 What's well-evidenced vs. speculative

Solid (cited primary data)

  • Compute scaling, training costs, revenue projections
  • Investment flows by country
  • Adoption rates (BTOS, Pew, Ramp)
  • Unemployment rate (BLS)

More speculative

  • GDP impact estimates (10-yr forecasts)
  • Whether scaling laws continue
  • Whether AI develops agency / replaces labor
  • CEA's policy impact estimates (own model)
