Overview & 5-Level Architecture

"Claude code builds beautiful websites. However, 95% of them are complete garbage. And that's not your fault. It's because Claude doesn't have the correct skills or knowledge. That's like having Mozart with no piano." [00:00:02]

Why "Mozart with no piano"?

Jack uses the Mozart analogy to argue that Claude is the talent (like Mozart) but lacks the instrument (skills, references, assets, data). Each level adds a new "instrument" so Mozart can actually perform.

Level 1 — Foundation

"They'll say something like, 'Hey there, Claude. I want you to build for me a simple website.' Okay, they just come over there. Yeah, they say, 'Claude, build me a website, please.' And the issue with this is that it's basically using Claude like a chatbot. The design quality is like three out of a hundred." [01:30-01:51]

What happens at L1

Treats Claude Code as a chatbot: "build me a website please." No context, no references, no assets. Result: 3/100 design quality, floating iPhone placeholder, "not too much happening in terms of creativity" [02:08].

Why it's still OK

Jack: "that's completely okay because this is only level one" [02:03]. Foundation is required — you have to start somewhere. The user gets a working skeleton.

Patrick skill mapping

No skill needed

L1 is the baseline. Any LLM can do this. Patrick skills apply starting at L2.

Level 2 — Screenshots & References

"Imagine, for example, having a designer, but instead of saying, 'Hey, make it look nice.' Instead, we get a beautiful sheet of paper and we say, 'Hey, I saw this apartment across the street and I love what they did here with the garden. Could we replicate that?' And here's a few other examples." [02:37-02:48]

Core method

Translate design taste into visual prompt + multiple analogies. Don't say "make it look nice" — say "I saw this, I love that, replicate this."

Why it works

Images encode design rules (color, layout, typography) that text prompts struggle to express. The designer analogy makes the request concrete — "apartment across the street" gives Claude a real reference.

Counter / Limits — BLIND SPOTs

Jack's video is high-energy and well-produced, but has several blind spots that a careful reader should not skip:

BLIND SPOT 1: "7 levels" promise vs 5 delivered

Jack opens with "I'll cover the seven levels" [00:00:14, 00:01:16, 00:54], but the video deep-dives 5 levels + briefly mentions L6, then ends at 18:00. L7 is unexplained. This is a clickbait-vs-content gap.

BLIND SPOT 2: "95% garbage" with no ground truth

The 95% statistic [00:00:02] is a hook with no sample size, no methodology, no source. It's a designed-to-go-viral opener, not a measured claim.

BLIND SPOT 3: L3 "not perfect" hand-waved

Jack admits L3 has stuff to change [07:39] but never specifies what. The "Bento Box style" is "in the right direction" but not finished.

BLIND SPOT 4: Visual sizzle ≠ substance

"Looks freaking amazing on the front of the house, but doesn't really do anything, okay?" [15:00]. Jack warns that visual polish without functionality is theater.

BLIND SPOT 5: Design rules hard to codify

"You can codify these rules, but it sometimes it's more difficult for you to explain." [18:18]. Jack admits golden ratio, typography, etc. are hard to prompt — the same gap he's trying to solve persists in L3-L6.

BLIND SPOT 6: Vendor lock-in (Firecrawl + OpenArt)

Jack recommends Firecrawl + OpenArt without discussing what happens when either raises prices, sunsets features, or gets acquired. Both are SaaS with no local fallback.

BLIND SPOT 7: No before/after metrics

Jack shows level transitions but never quantifies the quality lift (e.g. design score, conversion, user testing). Claims are all qualitative.
